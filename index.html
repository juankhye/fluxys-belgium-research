Section 01 · First Principles

What you need to understand before anything else

Fluxys is in a niche so specific that most investors have never priced it. Here's the mental model.

1

Gas isn't sold by Fluxys. Capacity is.

Fluxys does not buy or sell natural gas. It owns the pipes. Customers (energy traders, utilities, industrials) pay a tariff to book capacity on those pipes, much like booking seats on a train. Whether they actually flow gas or not, they pay. This is called "ship-or-pay".

2

It's a Transmission System Operator (TSO)

Belgium's gas system has three layers. Producers in Norway, Qatar, the US, etc. inject gas. Fluxys moves it across the country at high pressure (the "highway"). Distribution operators (Fluvius, Ores) deliver the gas at low pressure to your home (the "local roads"). Fluxys is the regulated middleman with no competition.

3

Belgium is wired into Europe — that's the alpha

Belgium itself only consumes 151 TWh of gas per year. But Fluxys's network transits 331 TWh through Belgium to neighbors — 214 TWh to Germany, 90 TWh to the Netherlands, 16 TWh to France. Since Russia stopped piping gas eastward, west-to-east transit volumes have surged.

4

Returns are capped — but also guaranteed

Belgian regulator CREG sets a 4-year tariff methodology. Tariffs are designed to fully cover all reasonable costs plus a fair return calculated as WACC + 100bp on the Regulated Asset Base (RAB). If volumes spike, the surplus is returned to customers via a regulatory adjustment account. Result: profitability is decoupled from volumes.

⚡ Mental Model #1 — Toll Road

Think of Fluxys as Belgium's gas toll road operator. Cars come and go (gas volumes shift). The toll operator doesn't care — it gets paid for available lane capacity regardless of traffic. The state says: "You can charge enough tolls to cover your costs plus a fair return on the road you built." That fair return is what guarantees the dividend.

⚡ Mental Model #2 — Bond With Optionality

The base business is essentially a fixed-income instrument — €1.40 per share dividend every year, mathematically dictated by the regulator. That's the bond. The optionality is the energy transition: Fluxys has been handed exclusive 20-year monopolies on Belgium's hydrogen and CO2 networks. If those scale, the RAB explodes.

⚡ Mental Model #3 — Why "Multi-Molecule" Matters

Fluxys's new pipelines are designed to carry any of three molecules: natural gas (CH4), hydrogen (H2), or carbon dioxide (CO2). One pipe, three potential revenue streams. As Europe decarbonizes, the gas itself changes — but the toll operator collects either way.

⚡ Mental Model #4 — The 2025 Bottom-Line "Drag" Is Actually a Feature

Net income fell to €74.9m in 2025 (vs €82.1m). Why? Fluxys is spending cash building H2 and CO2 pipes before the regulator sets tariffs for them. Today these are unremunerated "latent assets". Once the framework is finalized, they convert to RAB → triggering a positive earnings catch-up. Bears see a miss; bulls see a forced buying opportunity.

Section 04 · The Moat

Why nobody can build a competing gas pipeline in Belgium

There are three reasons the moat is essentially uncrossable. Plus a fourth that's actively being extended into hydrogen and CO2.

🏰

1. Capital Intensity

Building a parallel 4,000 km high-pressure gas grid would cost billions with no certainty of demand. There is zero economic or social rationale to support a competitor.

⚖️

2. Legal Lockout

EU's Third Energy Package + Belgium's national Gas Act statutorily designate one TSO per region. The legal barrier is absolute.

🔐

3. The Golden Share

The Belgian State holds a special "golden share" giving it veto rights over any sale of strategic infrastructure deemed to compromise national energy security.

🌐

4. Multi-Decade Mandates

Government decrees grant Fluxys exclusive 20-year operator mandates for the new H₂ backbone (Apr 2024) and regional CO₂ networks (2025-26) — locking competitors out before markets mature.

RAB economics — how the regulator determines profit

The Regulatory Formula

CREG's tariff methodology covers all reasonable operating costs plus a fair return on capital invested in the network. That return is anchored to the Belgian sovereign bond yield (the OLO).

Annual Profit = Average RAB × (WACC + 100bp)
Tariff Revenue = OPEX + Depreciation + Profit

If the company spends less than the budgeted OPEX, it keeps a portion of the saving (efficiency incentive). If transit volumes generate surplus revenue, that surplus is parked in a regulatory adjustment account and returned to customers — but the underlying regulated profit stays untouched.

What this means in practice

Regulatory tariffs were cut 10% on transmission and 20% on storage for the 2024-27 period. Bottom-line impact?

€0
Net income hit. The cost-plus model is structurally insulated from tariff changes — the regulator just recalibrates so OPEX + return are still covered.

EBITDA actually grew 5.9% to €320m in 2025, beating Kepler's €306m estimate, because the company captured efficiency incentives baked into the framework.

Section 08 · Investment Debate

Bull vs Bear — the polarized narrative

Most investors look at FLUX and see a sleepy regulated utility. The right framing isn't bull or bear — it's understanding what each side is actually arguing about.

▲The Bull Case

"A defensive yield trap that's mispriced by 35%."

  • ▸ Pure dividend play with peer-beating ~7% yield
    €1.40/share dividend held flat for four consecutive years (2022-25). Mathematically dictated by the regulator — completely insulated from gas volumes. Higher yield than Snam, Enagás, or Italgas with lower country risk.
  • ▸ 35% discount to RAB is structurally unjustified
    For a fully-regulated utility with a 4-year visible tariff regime + robust balance sheet + secured dividend coverage, a 35% RAB discount + implied 11% market WACC is "nonsensical" (Kepler).
  • ▸ Long-term RAB revival via the H₂/CO₂ pivot
    Latent assets currently dragging the bottom line will convert into regulated RAB once the framework is formalized — triggering a positive net income catch-up. Kepler models this revival but excludes it from base case (i.e. valuation today is "free" optionality).
  • ▸ Geopolitical immunity (the misunderstood risk)
    Fluxys is a tolling infrastructure provider — not a gas trader. Russian LNG sanctions, Strait of Hormuz disruptions, Qatari LNG cuts → all mean nothing financially. Long-term ship-or-pay contracts continue to bill regardless.
  • ▸ Path to net cash position by 2027 (ex-transition)
    Excluding the H₂/CO₂ outlays, base case projects Fluxys reaches a net cash position by FY27. The balance sheet is funding the transition, not stretching to do it.

▼The Bear Case

"A liquidity trap with a regulatory ceiling and political volatility."

  • ▸ Structural illiquidity is permanent
    10% free float, average daily volume under €0.1m. Most institutional investors structurally cannot own a meaningful position. Justifies a permanent liquidity discount (Kepler applies 25%) — and there's no path to fixing this without a parent-level decision.
  • ▸ Sovereign intervention risk just crystallized
    Belgian government's Nov 2025 budget announced intent to unilaterally withdraw €300m from regulatory adjustment accounts. Currently being contested. If executed, it's a violation of the "rules-based" framework that justified the low risk premium in the first place.
  • ▸ Capex / debt expansion beyond regulatory cover
    Net debt doubled from €159m to €327m in one year. Capex tripled to €262m. The H₂/CO₂ infrastructure is being built without finalized regulatory tariffs — if the framework is unfavorable or delayed, those assets sit in limbo.
  • ▸ Mature core market — RAB contraction risk
    Without the speculative H₂/CO₂ contribution, the base case actually projects ~2% annual contraction of the gas RAB. Belgian gas demand is structurally falling. The transition isn't optional — it's the only growth path.
  • ▸ Chicken-and-egg transition risk
    Fluxys is building H₂ and CO₂ pipelines today. But industrial demand for those molecules at scale won't materialize until the late 2020s/2030s. That's a 5-10 year period of capex carrying cost without revenue — and it depends on government support that just got cut by €250m for hydrogen.

⚖ The reconciled view

Both sides are correct on facts. The disagreement is about weighting. The bull says: the regulator has historically honored the framework, the dividend math is unbreakable, and the H₂/CO₂ optionality is undervalued. The bear says: the past does not bind a sovereign that has already announced intent to deviate, and unremunerated capex is a real cash drain. The right position size depends on confidence that the Belgian regulatory rules will hold.

Section 09 · Risks & Controversies

What can actually break the thesis

Three risks rated by severity. Two are tail events. One is structural.

High Severity · Active

Sovereign regulatory raid (€300m)

In its November 2025 budget, the Belgian federal government announced it would withdraw €300m from the positive balances of Fluxys's regulatory adjustment accounts. Management is formally contesting the legality. If executed, it directly damages the rules-based regulatory framework that the low-risk thesis depends on.

Watch: Court rulings, formal CREG response, potential precedent-setting impact on other Belgian utilities.

Medium Severity · Multi-year

Transition timing mismatch (chicken-and-egg)

Fluxys is spending €262m/year on H₂ and CO₂ pipelines now. Industrial offtake at scale is a 2028-2032 story. Hydrogen tariff framework still in development; regulator could set returns below the required hurdle rate. Belgian government already cut a €250m H₂ funding budget — a hostile signal.

Watch: Tariff methodology consultations from CREG; first H₂ industrial offtake contracts; CEF (Connecting Europe Facility) grant awards.

Medium Severity · Structural

Liquidity trap (10% free float)

Average daily volume <€0.1m means most large funds can't initiate or exit a meaningful position. Forces a permanent 25% liquidity discount in DCF models. Parent Fluxys SA owns 90% — only Fluxys SA could decide to release float. Single fund could move the share price violently.

Watch: Any Fluxys SA strategic review · Possible secondary offering (would be highly accretive). Otherwise, this is structurally permanent.

Low Severity · Misunderstood

EU sanctions on Russian LNG (14th package)

The trigger that drove FLUX to a 65% RAB discount at the lows. The 14th EU sanctions package (effective March 2025) prohibits transshipment of Russian LNG to non-EU countries via EU terminals. Fluxys management confirmed zero financial impact — Fluxys is a tolling provider, not an importer. Long-term ship-or-pay contracts pay regardless of physical flow.

Read: The retail panic created a buying opportunity, not a fundamental risk. Ironically, this is the discount that supports the bull case.

Low Severity · Insulated

Strait of Hormuz / Qatari LNG disruption

Iranian/Hormuz tensions in 2025-26 created concerns about Qatari LNG flows to Zeebrugge. Fluxys management confirmed: under "ship or pay" terminalling contracts, payment obligations remain in force regardless of supply fluctuations.

Read: Geographic supply risks always create headlines but rarely translate into Fluxys earnings risk.

Low Severity · Lagged

Interest rate / WACC adjustment lag

The 2024-27 tariff used a 1.68% risk-free rate at launch — quickly outdated by the higher-rate regime. Required complex re-consultations with CREG. Vast majority of debt is fixed, so cost-pass-through is fine eventually — but creates short-term mismatches.

Watch: 2027-2031 tariff period methodology · OLO yield trajectory.
